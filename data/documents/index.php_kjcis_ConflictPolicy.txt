Conflict of Interest Policy
A conflict of interest (COI) arises when personal, financial, or professional relationships could influence — or appear to influence — the work submitted for publication or the peer-review and editorial processes.
Conflicts of Interest — Authors
Authors must disclose all potential conflicts, including:
Financial interests (grants, funding, paid consultancies)
Institutional or organizational affiliations
Personal or professional relationships that may bias the research
Competing research or industry involvement
All disclosures must be included in a dedicated “Conflict of Interest” section within the manuscript. If there are no conflicts, authors must explicitly state:
“The authors declare no conflict of interest.”
Conflicts of Interest — Reviewers
Reviewers must decline review if they recognize a conflict (e.g., personal relationship, competitive research, shared affiliation).
Reviewers must not use information from the manuscript for personal advantage or research gain.
Conflicts of Interest — Editors
Editors must recuse themselves from manuscripts where a conflict exists.
Members of the editorial team cannot handle submissions in which they are authors.
The Editor-in-Chief may assign independent editors in cases of potential conflicts.
Institutional Responsibility
In cases where COI disclosure is unclear or suspected, the editorial office may seek clarification from authors or reviewers and consult respective institutions if required.
Corrective Actions
Failure to disclose conflicts may lead to:
Editorial rejection
Retraction of published articles
Notification to author institutions or funders
Conflict of Interest Statement
All manuscripts must include a COI statement according to journal policy before acceptance.
